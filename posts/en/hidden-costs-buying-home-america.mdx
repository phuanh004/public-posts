Most buyers focus on one number — the down payment. Save it, hand it over, move in. But the real cost of buying a home in America stretches well beyond that single check, and the buyers who feel "house poor" after closing are usually the ones who never saw the rest coming.

## The Down Payment Is Just the Beginning

A 20% down payment qualifies you for the loan. It does not put you in the home. Between the day you sign a purchase contract and the day you actually settle in, several other costs hit your account — some at closing, some monthly, some annually, and some only when something breaks.

The good news: every one of these costs is predictable. None of them are surprises if you know to ask.

## What to Budget for Beyond the Sticker Price

### 1. Closing Costs — Roughly 2-5% of the Purchase Price

Closing costs are the fees required to finalize the loan and transfer the property. On a $700,000 home, that's typically **$14,000 to $35,000**, due at the closing table. They include:

- Loan origination and underwriting fees
- Appraisal and credit report fees
- Title insurance and title search
- Escrow and recording fees
- Prepaid interest, taxes, and insurance

Some of these are negotiable. A skilled agent will push for seller credits where the market allows.

### 2. Property Taxes — A Yearly Bill That Never Goes Away

In California, property taxes run about **1.1-1.25% of the assessed value** every year. On a $700,000 home, that's **$7,700-$8,750 annually**, often collected monthly through your mortgage as part of an escrow account.

> Property taxes are not a one-time cost. They are a permanent line item — the price of owning, not buying.

### 3. Homeowners Insurance — Required by Every Lender

Expect to pay **$1,200-$2,500 per year** for a standard policy in San Diego, more in fire-prone or coastal zones. Lenders require it before they fund the loan, and they will collect it through escrow if you don't pay it directly.

### 4. Home Inspection — A Few Hundred Dollars That Save You Thousands

A general inspection costs **$400-$700** and is the single most important investment in due diligence. Specialty inspections (sewer line, roof, mold, pool, foundation) add **$150-$500 each** but are often worth every dollar — especially on older homes.

### 5. HOA Fees — Monthly, and They Rise

If the property is in a Homeowners Association, expect anywhere from **$200 to over $1,000 per month**, depending on amenities. HOA dues cover landscaping, common areas, sometimes water and roof maintenance — but they also include special assessments when reserves run short.

Always ask for the HOA's reserve study and recent meeting minutes before you commit.

### 6. Move-In, Repairs, and Furnishings

This is the cost most buyers underestimate. Painting, blinds, basic furniture, a refrigerator if it didn't convey, a new water heater the inspection flagged — it adds up fast. Set aside **1-3% of the purchase price** for the first year of ownership.

## The Real Question to Ask

Most buyers walk into a showing and ask, *"How much is this house?"* That number tells you almost nothing about whether you can afford it.

> Don't ask "how much does the house cost?" Ask "what does it cost to own this house?" — every month, every year, for as long as I plan to live here.

The total cost of ownership is the number that matters. It's the one your lender is calculating when they decide your debt-to-income ratio. It's the one that determines whether your home becomes a foundation for wealth — or a quiet source of stress.

## How to Prepare Before You Shop

A buyer who understands the full cost picture moves through the process with confidence. Here's how to get there:

- **Get pre-approved early** — your lender will outline the realistic monthly payment including taxes and insurance
- **Build a closing-cost reserve** — 3-5% of your target purchase price, separate from the down payment
- **Ask for a sample Loan Estimate** before you write an offer — it itemizes every fee
- **Request HOA documents** the moment you tour a community property
- **Plan a maintenance fund** — 1% of home value per year is a safe rule
